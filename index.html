Poultry Basics

Chicken, Rooster, or Hen?

Before exploring how they reproduce, let's clear up the common confusion between these basic terms!

The Chicken

The Species

Chicken is the general name for the entire bird species (Gallus gallus domesticus). Whether it is a baby, a male, or a female, they are all chickens!

Includes chicks, pullets, cockerels, hens, and roosters.

The Hen

Adult Female (1+ Years)

A Hen is a fully mature female chicken. Hens are the ones that lay eggs, hatch chicks, and go "broody" to sit on nests. They are generally quieter and smaller.

Young females under 1 year are called Pullets.

The Rooster

Adult Male (1+ Years)

A Rooster (or cock) is a mature male chicken. They don't lay eggs! Instead, they fertilize eggs, protect the flock from predators, and crow loudly to establish territory.

Young males under 1 year are called Cockerels.

The 5 Core Stages of the Reproductive Cycle

Let's follow the natural progress from when a rooster and hen meet, to how an egg is developed, incubated, and hatched.

1

Mating & Genetics

A rooster courts a hen with a dance. For an egg to hatch, sperm must fertilize the germinal disc at the top of the egg yolk.

2

Egg Assembly

Over 25 hours, a hen produces a single egg. High-calcium shell glands coat the egg before it's laid.

3

Nesting Instinct

Once she gathers a clutch (typically 10-12 eggs), the hen goes "broody," remaining on the nest to keep them warm.

4

Incubation

Takes 21 days of strict care. Eggs need a constant temp of 99.5°F (37.5°C), high humidity, and regular turning.

5

The Hatch!

The chick uses a special temporary egg tooth on its beak to break through the shell, zipping around and rolling free!

Do Hens Need a Rooster to Lay Eggs?

Fun Fact: No! A hen's body releases an egg yolk regularly, regardless of whether a rooster is present. The standard backyard chicken will lay tasty, unfertilized eggs which can never hatch. However, if a rooster is in the flock, he will mate with the hen, fertilizing the tiny white spot on the yolk (the germinal disc), allowing it to develop into a chick once incubated.
Key Anatomical Roles:

The Yolk:

Contains the fat, proteins, and vitamins needed to feed the growing embryo for 21 days.

Albumen (Egg White):

Composed of 90% water and protective proteins, buffering the yolk from physical shock.

Chalaza:

Rope-like structures made of twisted protein strands that anchor the yolk perfectly in the center of the egg.

The Shell & Air Cell:

The calcium shell contains thousands of microscopic pores allowing air inside. The air cell at the bottom expands as water evaporates.
